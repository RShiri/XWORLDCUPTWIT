xG Efficiency Lab

Do teams score as much as the quality of their chances says they should? These charts compare expected goals (xG) with what actually happened.

New here? What is xG?

Expected goals (xG) rates every shot by how likely it was to be scored, from 0 to 1. A tap-in from the six-yard box might be 0.7 xG (scored ~70% of the time); a hopeful shot from 30 metres might be 0.03 xG (3%). Add up a team's shots and you get how many goals an average finisher would have scored from those chances.

So if a team racks up 2.5 xG but scores 1, they created good chances and wasted them (or met a great keeper). Score 3 from 1.0 xG and they finished brilliantly — or got lucky. Over a few games xG tends to predict results better than the scoreline alone, which is why it's the go-to "who deserved it" number.

Note: where the official xG wasn't published, we estimate it from each shot's location and type using the same model that draws the match infographics — those games are tagged est.

Did chances turn into goals?

Every dot is one team's performance in one match: how many goals an average team would expect from their chances (across →) versus how many they actually scored (up ↑). The dashed line is "scored exactly what the chances were worth". Above the line = clinical finishing; below = chances wasted. The green line is the overall trend.

How closely do goals follow xG?

If xG were perfect, goals would track it exactly. This measures how strong that link has been so far — and how much finishing skill or luck is adding noise on top.

Finishing: goals scored minus xG

For each team, total goals minus total xG. Green = clinical (scored more than their chances were worth — sharp finishing or good luck); red = wasteful (scored fewer — poor finishing or unlucky). Around zero means they scored about what was expected.

Who's good at both ends? — attack vs. defence quality

Each team is placed by how dangerous its attack is (further right = more/better chances created per game) and how mean its defence is (higher up = fewer/worse chances allowed per game). The dashed lines are the tournament average, splitting the pitch into four types of team — the colour key below explains each. Top-right = strong both ways; bottom-left = leaky and blunt.

Are teams getting the points they deserve?

Expected points turns each game's xG into the points an average team would win from those chances. Compare it with real points: above the line = winning more than the performances merit (clinical or lucky); below = deserved more than they've got.

Quality vs quantity of shots

Shots per game (across →) vs average xG per shot (up ↑ = better chances). Top-right = lots of good chances; top-left = few but excellent looks; bottom-right = high-volume long-range shooting; bottom-left = struggling to create anything.

Is there a home advantage?

Average xG created by teams listed as "home" vs "away". A clear gap suggests the designated home side tends to create better chances — the classic home advantage, in chance-quality terms.

Team-by-team xG ledger

The full breakdown — click any column header to sort. xG = quality of chances created; xGA = quality of chances allowed; G−xG = attacking finishing (＋ = clinical); xGA−GA = goalkeeping/defending vs the chances faced (＋ = kept out more than expected); xGD = xG minus xGA, i.e. how much a team out-chanced opponents overall ("deserved" goal difference).

Did the team that "deserved" it actually win?

For every game with xG, we check whether the side with more xG (the better chances) got the result. A high match rate means xG is tracking reality; mismatches (✘) are the games that defied the underlying play — often decided by a worldie, a penalty, or a red card.

Unluckiest results (deserved more)

Games where the side that out-created their opponent by the most xG still failed to win — they made the better chances but didn't get the result. The biggest "should have won this" performances.

Power Rank & Knockout Predictions

A single Power Index for the 32 teams in the Round of 32, blending each side's pre-tournament FIFA ranking with how they actually played in the group stage (points won, goal difference and expected-goal difference). Those ratings then drive a model that simulates every knockout tie — all the way to a predicted champion.

How the Power Index & predictions work

Every team starts from its FIFA/Coca-Cola World Ranking points (the 11 June 2026 list, the last update before kick-off). We then add a form adjustment of up to ±100 points from the group stage: points per game, goal difference per game, and xG difference per game (chance quality created minus allowed). The result is the Power Index below.

For each knockout tie we turn the two teams' Power Index gap into a win probability with an Elo-style curve (a 100-point edge ≈ 64%, 200 ≈ 74%). The higher-rated side is projected to advance, with a likely scoreline derived from the same gap and each team's scoring form. Because it's a knockout, the model always picks a winner — level games are decided "on penalties" by the higher Power Index. It's a deterministic projection, not a probability over every possible bracket: real tournaments throw up upsets the favourites-advance path can't.

FIFA ranking source: the FIFA/Coca-Cola Men's World Ranking published 11 June 2026 (Argentina 1st on 1877 pts). Points for the lowest-ranked sides are approximate. Form & xG come from this site's own scraped group-stage data.
