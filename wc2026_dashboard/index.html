xG Efficiency Lab

Do teams score as much as the quality of their chances says they should? These charts compare expected goals (xG) with what actually happened.

New here? What is xG?

Expected goals (xG) rates every shot by how likely it was to be scored, from 0 to 1. A tap-in from the six-yard box might be 0.7 xG (scored ~70% of the time); a hopeful shot from 30 metres might be 0.03 xG (3%). Add up a team's shots and you get how many goals an average finisher would have scored from those chances.

So if a team racks up 2.5 xG but scores 1, they created good chances and wasted them (or met a great keeper). Score 3 from 1.0 xG and they finished brilliantly — or got lucky. Over a few games xG tends to predict results better than the scoreline alone, which is why it's the go-to "who deserved it" number.

Where does our xG come from? We built the model ourselves. Every shot is scored by a blend of logistic regression and gradient boosting, trained on ~78,000 real shots — four seasons each of La Liga and the Premier League, plus this World Cup. It weighs the shot's distance, the angle the goal mouth opens up from that spot, whether it was a header, an Opta big chance, the situation (open play, free kick, corner, set piece, fast break), and context like a one-on-one, a cross or a through ball. A per-competition calibration keeps summed xG in line with goals actually scored — World Cup finishing runs hotter than league play, and the calibration accounts for it (penalties are fixed at 0.79).

Expected assists (xA) works the same way for passes: every completed pass is scored on how likely it was to directly set up a goal, trained on 2.4 million real passes. A teammate's shot doesn't need to actually go in for the pass to earn credit — a great through-ball that gets wasted still counts, the same way a great chance still counts as good xG even if it's missed.

Where official xG is also published (e.g. FotMob), the Matches list shows both side by side — Model and FotMob — rather than blending them, so you can compare directly.

Power Rank & Knockout Predictions

A single Power Index for the 32 teams in the Round of 32, blending each side's pre-tournament FIFA ranking with how they've actually played — recency-weighted form, finishing & shot-stopping quality, and squad quality (best-XI average rating). Those ratings then drive a model that simulates every knockout tie — all the way to a predicted champion.

How the Power Index & predictions work

Every team starts from its FIFA/Coca-Cola World Ranking points (the 11 June 2026 list, the last update before kick-off). Three capped adjustments sit on top of that baseline:

1. Form (±100 pts) — points per game (group stage), plus goal difference and xG difference per game computed across every match played so far, group and knockout alike, with a recency weighting (half-life ~4 games) so a team's most recent result counts for more than its opener. Folded into the same term: finishing & shot-stopping quality (±20 pts) — goals scored vs xG, and goals conceded vs xG allowed — rewarding clinical finishing and last-ditch goalkeeping beyond raw chance volume.

2. Squad (±55 pts) — a "best XI" proxy: each team's 11 most-used players this tournament (by minutes played), their match ratings averaged and minutes-weighted, compared against the 48-team field average.

For each knockout tie we turn the two teams' Power Index gap into a win probability with an Elo-style curve (a 100-point edge ≈ 64%, 200 ≈ 74%). The higher-rated side is projected to advance, with a likely scoreline derived from the same gap and each team's scoring form. Because it's a knockout, the model always picks a winner — level games are decided "on penalties" by the higher Power Index. Once a tie is actually played, the real result replaces the projection and feeds forward into the next round — this is a deterministic projection for what hasn't happened yet, not a probability over every possible bracket: real tournaments throw up upsets the favourites-advance path can't.

FIFA ranking source: the FIFA/Coca-Cola Men's World Ranking published 11 June 2026 (Argentina 1st on 1877 pts). Points for the lowest-ranked sides are approximate. Form, xG, quality and squad ratings all come from this site's own scraped match & player data.

Standouts — Every Dot Is a Player

Pick a stat and see the whole tournament's distribution at once. The curve is how all players spread out, the dashed line is the average, and each dot is one player. The names way out on the right are the anomalies — the ones breaking from the pack.

How to read this

This is a density plot: where the curve is tall, lots of players sit on that value; where it's flat, hardly anyone does. Most players cluster near the average, so the curve peaks there and tails off. A player far to the right of the crowd is doing something rare — we measure exactly how rare in σ (sigma) over average: +1σ beats ~84% of players, +2σ beats ~98%, +3σ is a genuine outlier.

Dots are coloured pink once a player is 2σ or more above average. Pick a player to spotlight them in gold and see their percentile.

Cooling Breaks

What the two cooling breaks (~min 23 and ~min 74) actually do to a game. Break minutes are detected from dead gaps in the event stream; momentum blends window xG, field tilt and possession, measured against the churn of ordinary 7-minute windows. Spoiler: the breaks move games less than the headlines claim — and most of the “leader loses momentum” effect is regression to the mean (the grey control below shows how much).
